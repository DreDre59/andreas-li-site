Designing your first PCB is one of those rites of passage in embedded engineering. You spend hours in KiCad feeling like a wizard, send it off to JLCPCB, and then spend the next two weeks praying you didn't swap TX and RX on the UART header.

I did swap TX and RX. This post is about the other mistakes too.

## Mistake 1: Ignoring Decoupling Capacitor Placement

I knew I needed decoupling caps — 100nF on every VCC pin, standard stuff. What I didn't understand was that **placement matters as much as the value**. My first board had the caps at the edge of the board, connected to power pins via long traces.

<Callout type="warning">
  A decoupling cap 5cm from the IC it's supposed to decouple is almost useless. The inductance of the trace negates the benefit.
</Callout>

The fix is simple: place decoupling caps as close as possible to the power pin, with the shortest possible trace to both the pad and the ground via.

## Mistake 2: No Testpoints

When something didn't work, I had nowhere to probe. I was trying to touch a 0402 component with oscilloscope probes and losing my mind.

Now I add at least a via on every net I might want to debug — they're free, they're small, and they will save you hours.

## Mistake 3: Wrong Footprint on a Crystal

I pulled a crystal footprint from a community library without checking the datasheet. The pad dimensions were off by about 0.3mm. The crystal sort of held on after soldering but it was ugly and mechanically fragile.

**Lesson:** Always verify footprints against the actual datasheet. Always.

## What I Got Right

Not everything was a disaster:

- My power supply section actually worked first try (luck? careful calculation? probably both)
- The board was single-sided with wide traces, which made hand assembly reasonable
- I included a reset button and a power LED — simple things that saved debugging time

## Checklist I Use Now

Before sending any design out for fab:

- [ ] Run DRC with zero errors
- [ ] Check every footprint against its datasheet
- [ ] Verify all decoupling cap placement
- [ ] Add testpoints on key nets
- [ ] Double-check connector pinouts against the mating header
- [ ] Check minimum trace width for current carrying capacity
- [ ] Verify board dimensions against enclosure

It's not a perfect list but it's caught problems before they became expensive.
